In this notebook, I develop a model for predicting the risk of a county having residents die of a fentanyl overdose.

The data used to build the model comes from the CDC WONDER detailed mortality database, the American Community Survey, and the CDC's database on opioid prescription rates.

I define the problem as predicting which counties with zero deaths will have more than zero deaths the following year. Counties with more than zero deaths are automatically classified as high risk, so the challenge is figuring out which counties will start to have a fentanyl problem before any deaths actually occur. To do this, I trained a model on the data from 2016 for counties that have zero deaths, with the label being a 0 if the number of deaths stayed 0 in 2017 or a 1 if there were deaths in 2017. I favor recall when training the model, it is more important to identify more counties at risk than it is to be accurate.

My final model has a recall of 73% and an accuracy of 87%, meaning the model should be able to predict 73% of the counties that are at risk of developing a fentanyl problem next year. These at risk counties could be given more naloxone to combat the problem and should make sure their staff and residents are trained.
